Maori Traditions have been passed down for centuries and resemble the values and morals that their ancestors carried with them. This is a very special aspect of Maori Culture as it provides guidance for younger generations in how to act and be respectful in all situations.

These traditions are seen in a variety of situations from important events on a Marae, gatherings to mourn those who have passed, or just general guidelines in everyday life.

Karakia (Prayer) is said before eating and before meetings / ceremonies.

When leaving an Urupa (cemetery), Maori cleanse themselves with water to return to the land of the living.

It is disrespectful to sit or place your feet on pillows and tables as it is where food and heads should go, not anything else.

Shoes and hats must be removed upon entering Wharenui (main house).

“Ina kei te mohio koe ko wai koe, I anga mai koe I hea, kei te mohio koe, kei te anga atu ki hea”

- If you know who you are and where you are from, then you will know where you are going

Māori Culture celebrates the significance of all Polynesian cultures and their unique traditions. Māori Culture also represents the visions of Māori and their ambitions for the future through their traditions.
